Autonomous UAV Trajectory Planning & Control

Jan 2024 - Present
Control Systems Researcher
Vancouver, BC
Algorithms, Optimization, and Control Lab (AOCL), UBC

View Details

In this project, I developed a real‑time trajectory planning and control system for long‑range fixed‑wing UAVs operating over complex terrain and in dynamic, wind‑affected environments under strict onboard energy constraints. My work addresses the challenge of achieving energy‑aware, certifiable autonomy for critical missions such as medical logistics and remote environmental monitoring, where communication links and airspace permissions are uncertain.

Figure. (Semi)autonomous long-range fixed-wing UAVs are increasingly deployed across a wide range of applications.


Figure. Other methods people have proposed for solving this problem.


Modeling & Simulation

I build a high‑fidelity 4‑DOF fixed‑wing UAV model with coupled kinematic and energy dynamics, incorporating drag‑polar‑based power models and battery state‑of‑charge constraints.

Figure. coupled kinematic and energy model.


The dynamics integrate realistic wind fields, terrain elevation data, and altitude‑dependent air density to accurately capture wind–terrain–energy interactions during long‑range cruise.

Figure. Visual representations of the terrain and no-fly zone topography of the simulated UAV environment, where the axes represent ground-plane position and elevation in meters above sea level. The initial UAV position (yellow circle) and target region center (red square). (a) Side view of mountainous and valley terrain with satellite image. (b) Top view with terrain surface elevation.


Control Architecture & Planning Algorithm

Figure. Overview of the trajectory planning and control framework. The planner continuously receives updated state and environment information and re-plans, sending updated reference waypoints to the controller for online adaptation.


The long range mission planning problem is reformulated as a finite horizon MDP over a pre stabilized closed loop system, augmenting the state with reference/waypoint variables for constraint handling.

Figure. Reformulated MDP and pre-stabilizing controllers.


An Explicit Reference Governor–style navigation field is employed to enforce terrain, altitude, and no fly zone constraints at the reference level while preserving controller stability.

Figure. Principle of the navigation field: the applied reference v avoids obstacles by moving along the field direction, which attracts it toward the desired target r while repelling it from nearby constraints.


Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS)–based replanning framework is designed that searches in reference space, with low level feedback controllers handling guidance, tracking, and collision avoidance.

Figure. Flowchart of the proposed MCTS planner procedure. Through repeated iterations, the planner incrementally refines its search tree, which naturally grows denser in high-reward regions, converging toward a near-optimal reference policy.


Combining MPC style receding horizon operation with tree reuse (subtree warm starting) enables fast, onboard replanning under evolving wind and airspace constraints.

Figure. Illustration of tree reuse during closed-loop re-planning. The retained subtree (blue nodes) rooted at the current best trajectory branch provides an informed initialization for the next MCTS iteration, reducing computation time.


Implementation & Results

The full planning and control stack is implemented in MATLAB as a modular, object oriented codebase (simulation environment, MCTS core, controllers, constraint and environment modules).

Initial flight plan: Compared with baseline algorithm (BIT*), MCTS planner generates wind aware, energy efficient long range trajectories that outperform in remaining energy at arrival, despite slightly longer path length.

Figure. Comparison of flight plans and performance (BIT* (green), MCTS (blue and red)).


Figure. Exploration patterns of the MCTS search trees and reward convergence comparison.


Online re-planning: Achieved fast online replanning under dynamic constraints (emerging no fly zones, changing wind forecasts) while maintaining feasibility, terrain clearance, and regulatory airspace separation.

Figure. Online re-planning under an emerging storm front. Each panel shows the nominal pre-flight trajectory (red), the current MCTS-generated plan (blue) , and the storm-front boundary (crimson surface) at the corresponding re-planning time.


Figure. Visualization of MCTS search trees and sampled trajectories (grey) during online re-planning.


Figure. Quantitative performance metrics for online re-planning. (a)Cumulative reward evolution across re-planning cycles.(b) Onboard energy consumption: actual trajectory vs. original plan.(c) Altitude profile: actual trajectory vs. original plan.


Demonstrated robustness across extensive simulation scenarios, showcasing skills at the intersection of aerodynamics, control, and algorithm design.
